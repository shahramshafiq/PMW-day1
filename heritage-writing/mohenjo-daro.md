# Mohenjo-daro: A City Running Out of Time

**Shahram Shafiq | Special Session: Research Methods, Sourcing & Writing (w2d3)**

*Writing prompt: 300-500 words on a space, what it is, why it matters, what a visitor should notice. Every fact checked against a real source.*

---

Mohenjo-daro sits on the west bank of the lower Indus River in Larkana District, Sindh. It was built around 2500 BCE and is one of the largest settlements of the Indus Valley Civilization, spread across roughly 300 hectares and estimated to have housed at least 40,000 people at its peak [1]. It was rediscovered by R. D. Banerji of the Archaeological Survey of India in 1919 and 1920, and in 1980 it became the first site in South Asia to be named a UNESCO World Heritage Site [1].

What makes Mohenjo-daro remarkable is not a single monument, it is the ordinariness of its infrastructure, engineered 4,500 years before "infrastructure" was a word anyone needed. The city is laid out on a grid, with streets running in straight lines and houses built from standardized burnt brick. It had over 700 wells, roughly one for every three houses, and a centralized drainage system running alongside the streets that carried wastewater out of the city instead of letting it pool [1]. At the center of the site sits the Great Bath, a sunken pool roughly 12 by 7 meters and 2.4 meters deep, waterproofed with bitumen, which archaeologists believe served a ceremonial or civic purpose rather than a purely practical one [1]. A visitor standing over it today is looking at what is likely the oldest known public water tank in the ancient world.

Why it matters right now, urgently, is that Mohenjo-daro is disappearing. The same burnt brick that survived 4,500 years is being destroyed by groundwater salinity: capillary action pulls salt-laden moisture up through the walls, and repeated wetting and drying cycles, worsened by summer heat above 50 degrees Celsius and monsoon rain, crystallize salt inside the brick until it crumbles [2]. In 2012, Pakistani archaeologists warned that without a major new conservation effort, the site could disappear entirely by 2030 [2]. That warning was not abstract: in 2022, exceptional monsoon rainfall critically damaged several parts of the site, and UNESCO sent emergency conservation missions in November 2022 and again in March 2023 [2].

A visitor today should notice two things at once: the astonishing ordinariness of a well-planned ancient city, drains, wells, and brick houses, nothing performative about any of it, and the visible decay eating into that same brick right now. That tension is exactly why this site matters to a 3D reconstruction project. A digital record will not stop the salt, but it can make sure that what is currently standing does not simply vanish from memory the way the city's original name already has. "Mohenjo-daro" is a modern Sindhi name meaning "Mound of the Dead Men," not what the people who built it called their own city.
